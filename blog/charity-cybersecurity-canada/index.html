Canadian charity cybersecurity: what every registered charity needs in 2026

Most Canadian registered charities are operating at what the sector calls a ground-zero security posture. This post explains what changed in 2026, what the baseline now looks like, and a specific first-30-days plan.

April 15, 2026 · Redact Labs · 12 min read

Canadian registered charities face the same cybersecurity threats as corporations — phishing, ransomware, account takeover, donor-data exposure — but with a fraction of the budget and almost none of the internal IT capacity. This guide is a practical walkthrough of what the baseline looks like in 2026, why it has shifted, and a specific, costed first-30-days plan for charities starting from scratch.

Why the sector's starting posture is unusually low

Most Canadian charities — including well-run, well-governed organisations — are operating at what our practice calls a ground-zero security posture. The characteristics are consistent across the sector:

  • No multi-factor authentication (MFA) on email, banking, or the donor database
  • No written Acceptable Use Policy; no board-level accountability for cybersecurity
  • Donor data scattered across Excel spreadsheets, personal Gmail accounts, and Dropbox folders whose owners left years ago
  • Shared passwords ("everyone knows the Mailchimp login")
  • Former staff and board members still active in systems
  • Paper records with PII in unlocked filing cabinets
  • No incident-response plan, no phone tree, no idea who to call if something goes wrong

This is not negligence. It's the predictable result of three structural factors: (1) charity operating budgets rarely accommodate IT, (2) charities grow organically without a "now we're big enough for governance" moment, and (3) the sector has historically received almost no cyber-specific guidance from its regulators or umbrella bodies.

What changed in 2026

Three shifts have made the status quo untenable:

1. Attackers have automated their work

Ten years ago, attackers chose targets. Today, they use automated tools that probe every internet-connected organisation — charity, corporation, or individual — within days of any new vulnerability being disclosed. There is no "we're too small to be interesting" bucket anymore. A charity with poor MFA hygiene is found and exploited by scripts, not humans.

2. Cyber insurance underwriters have tightened requirements

Charities applying for or renewing cyber insurance in 2026 are being asked detailed questions about MFA coverage, endpoint protection, backup strategy, and incident response capability. Organisations that can't answer these questions are either being declined outright or quoted premiums that eat into programme budgets. The underwriter questionnaire has become a de-facto compliance checklist.

3. Funders are asking the questions too

Major Canadian foundations and government funders are increasingly including cybersecurity questions in grant applications — particularly where grant data includes beneficiary PII or where the grant exceeds a threshold. CyberSecure Canada (the federal baseline certification) is appearing as an eligibility criterion in RFPs.

4. Donor expectations have shifted

Sector surveys have consistently shown that a meaningful portion of donors — around 28% in recent Canadian polling — say they would stop giving to a charity that suffered a breach. Charity sustainability is increasingly tied to demonstrated trustworthiness with personal data.

What the baseline looks like in 2026

Every Canadian charity, regardless of size, should meet a minimum baseline. We use two framings, depending on the audience:

For technical readers: CIS Implementation Group 1 (IG1)

The CIS Critical Security Controls IG1 is the global baseline — 56 safeguards across 18 control families. It's what every organisation should meet, regardless of size. For a ground-zero charity, meeting even 50% of IG1 is a transformational improvement.

For boards: the NIST CSF current-state profile

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 organises cybersecurity into six functions (Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover), each scored on a five-tier maturity scale (Tier 0 to Tier 4). A typical ground-zero charity scores Tier 0 across the board. After a well-executed stabilisation programme, the profile lifts to Tier 1 across all six — a posture most boards recognise as "meeting our obligations."

The specific controls every charity needs

Regardless of framing, the concrete list is short. These are the controls that move the needle:

Baseline controls (non-negotiable)

  • Multi-factor authentication on email, banking, donor database, payment processor, file storage, and social media accounts the charity controls
  • Named user accounts — no shared logins; when someone leaves, their access is removed within 24 hours
  • Password manager (Bitwarden or 1Password) deployed to every staff member; no passwords in spreadsheets or sticky notes
  • Written Acceptable Use Policy — one page, board-approved, signed by staff, board, and volunteers
  • Incident-response phone tree — printed, laminated, posted; top numbers tested annually
  • Independent backup of Microsoft 365 or Google Workspace data — daily, 90-day retention minimum, tested quarterly
  • Endpoint protection (Microsoft Defender for Business or Bitdefender GravityZone) on every device that touches charity data
  • DNS filtering to block known malware and phishing domains (Cloudflare Gateway is free)
  • Security awareness training — quarterly phishing simulations, monthly micro-training
  • Access inventory — documented list of every system and every person who has access, reviewed quarterly

What's striking about this list is that none of it requires expensive tooling. The entire baseline can be implemented on nonprofit-tier pricing with costs typically under $100/user/year.

A specific first-30-days plan

If your charity has never done any of this, here's exactly where to start. This plan is designed to be doable by a non-technical executive director with a few hours a week, plus a friendly volunteer with some IT background.

Week 1: Inventory

  1. Open a blank spreadsheet. Two tabs: "Systems" and "People".
  2. Under Systems, list every tool the charity uses: email, donor database, accounting, payment processor, mailing list, file storage, social media, banking, grant-management portals.
  3. Under People, list every staff member, board member, and active volunteer. Next to each, list which Systems they have access to, and at what level (admin, user, view-only).
  4. Flag anyone no longer associated with the charity who might still have access anywhere. These are your first priority for account deactivation.

Week 2: Turn on MFA everywhere

  1. Start with email. If you use Microsoft 365, enable Security Defaults in the admin centre. If you use Google Workspace, enable 2-Step Verification enforcement.
  2. Banking and payment processor: turn on MFA. Every authorised signatory.
  3. Donor database: every user gets MFA.
  4. File storage: every user gets MFA.
  5. Social media accounts the charity controls: MFA on each, with recovery emails pointing to the charity email (not personal).

This single step is the highest-impact cybersecurity action a charity can take. It doesn't require a vendor, a contract, or a budget.

Week 3: Write and sign the AUP

The Acceptable Use Policy is a one-page document that says:

  • Charity business is done through charity email and charity file storage only — not personal Gmail, not WhatsApp, not text messages
  • Every person has their own login; nobody shares passwords
  • MFA is on for email, banking, donor system, file storage, and payment processor
  • When someone leaves, all their accounts are deactivated within 24 hours
  • If something looks wrong — a phishing email you clicked, a lost laptop, a strange login alert — call the incident point of contact immediately; don't try to fix it yourself

Get it to your board for approval. Have every staff, board, and volunteer sign it. Keep signed copies in the charity's file storage.

Week 4: Build and test the incident phone tree

One page. Real names and numbers. Five steps: (1) executive director and backup, (2) board chair and treasurer, (3) your IT contact or cyber firm plus insurance broker, (4) legal counsel and bank for confirmed incidents, (5) regulators (Privacy Commissioner, IPC, Quebec Commission d'accès) within 72 hours of confirmed breach of personal information.

Print it. Laminate it. Post one in the office. Test the top three numbers (call them, leave a "this is a test" voicemail). Put a recurring calendar reminder on to retest annually.

What this costs

The first 30 days, done in-house by charity staff with a volunteer's help: $0–$200 (password manager licences, optionally).

Done with external help (like Redact Labs' Tier 0 engagement): $3,000–$15,000 fixed-fee, four-week engagement. The variable is charity size; a three-person grassroots charity and a 40-person regional organisation are very different scopes.

What comes next

Once the 30-day baseline is in place, the next layer is ongoing: endpoint protection, backup, DNS filtering, security awareness training, and a quarterly review cadence. This is where managed-service tiers (Essentials, Secure, Guardian) come in. But there's no point in layering tooling on top of a missing foundation — get the baseline done first.
